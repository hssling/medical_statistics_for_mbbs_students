1.1 What is Medical Statistics?

Key Definition

Medical statistics is the science of collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting healthcare data to make informed medical and health decisions.

Medical statistics provides the tools to:

  • Quantify the uncertainty in medical research findings
  • Determine if differences between treatments are real or due to chance
  • Calculate the probability of outcomes
  • Design studies that can answer important clinical questions

Clinical Example

A hospital administrator wants to know if a new patient education program reduces readmission rates. Statistical analysis can determine whether the observed decrease in readmissions is likely due to the program rather than random variation.

1.2 Role in Evidence-Based Medicine

Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) follows this process:

  1. Ask a focused clinical question
  2. Acquire the best available evidence
  3. Appraise the evidence critically
  4. Apply the results to patient care
  5. Assess the outcomes

Statistics enables all steps of EBM by providing tools to:

  • Assemble evidence from multiple studies (meta-analysis)
  • Critically evaluate research methods
  • Apply research findings to individual patients
  • Monitor results over time

1.3 Types of Data in Healthcare

Categorical Data

  • Nominal: Categories without order (blood type, gender)
  • Ordinal: Categories with meaningful order (pain scale, cancer stage)
  • Binary: Two categories (alive/dead, yes/no)

Numerical Data

  • Continuous: Any value within a range (blood pressure, weight)
  • Discrete: Whole numbers (number of children, hospital visits)

Time-to-Event Data

Time until an event occurs (survival time, time to disease recurrence)

1.5 The Scientific Method in Medical Research

  1. Identify the Problem: Observe or question existing practice
  2. Review Literature: What is already known?
  3. Formulate Hypothesis: Make a testable prediction
  4. Design Study: Choose appropriate research methods
  5. Collect Data: Systematically gather information
  6. Analyze Data: Use statistical methods
  7. Interpret Results: Draw conclusions from the data
  8. Report Findings: Share with the scientific community

1.6 Common Pitfalls in Medical Statistics

Statistical Pitfalls

  • Drawing conclusions beyond the data: Although statistics can demonstrate difference, it cannot prove causation
  • Ignoring sampling bias: Results from non-representative samples may not apply to the broader population
  • Et Tu Brute sums: No single test reveals the complete truth about complex medical phenomena

To avoid these pitfalls:

  • Always consider alternative explanations
  • Calculate confidence intervals as well as p-values
  • Carefully examine study design and samples
  • Consider biological plausibility of results

1.7 Ethical Considerations

Research ethics ensure the responsible conduct of studies:

  • Informed consent: Participants understand risks and benefits
  • Beneficence: Research must benefit participants and society
  • Non-maleficence: First, do no harm
  • Justice: Fair distribution of research benefits and burdens
  • Confidentiality: Protect participant privacy

Institutional Review Boards (IRBs)

IRBs review research proposals to ensure ethical compliance. Studies cannot begin without IRB approval.

Chapter Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Medical statistics applies statistical methods to healthcare problems
  • Statistics is essential for evidence-based medicine
  • Understanding data types helps choose appropriate analyses
  • The scientific method provides a systematic approach to research
  • Careful attention to ethics ensures responsible research

What You Learned:

  • Distinguish between different types of data
  • Understand the difference between populations and samples
  • Identify common statistical pitfalls
  • Recognize ethical considerations in medical research
